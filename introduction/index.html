Degree

The degree of a node represents the number of connections it has. For example, a high degree node in a social network can signify a popular individual. Similarly, a research paper with a significantly large degree might be influential in the field. The degree of a node iii is designated as kik_iki​.

For an undirected graph, the degree of a node is simply the number of nodes it is adjacent to. Moreover, the total number of edges ∣E∣|E|∣E∣ can also be derived by summing the degree of every node and halving the sum to account for the fact that each edge is counted twice.

Graph Connectivity

One of the most common questions you can ask about a network is whether nodes uuu and vvv are connected or not. For example, if you are planning a road trip you need to know if it's even possible to reach your destination by car. Graph connectivity is also related to the resilience of a network – a power grid that requires the destruction of 20 power plants to cause a total blackout is much more robust than one that disconnects after one explosion. Therefore, let's first formally define what we mean by connectivity.

A path from uuu to vvv is finite sequence of distinct edges that begin at uuu and end at vvv. A graph is said to be connected if there is a path between any two vertices. For example, the following graph is connected because the paths (1,3)(1,3)(1,3), (1,2)(1,2)(1,2) and (3,1),(1,2)(3,1),(1,2)(3,1),(1,2) connect every pair of vertices.
